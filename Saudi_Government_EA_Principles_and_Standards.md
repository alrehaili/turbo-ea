# Enterprise Architecture Principles and Standards Catalog

## Saudi Government Authority — NORA-Aligned Draft

**Document status:** Proposed enterprise catalog for review and formal adoption  
**Prepared:** 15 July 2026  
**Primary context:** Saudi government authority  
**EA method:** National Overall Reference Architecture (NORA)  
**Language:** English; official Arabic legal and regulatory texts prevail where applicable

> **Important status note:** This is a professionally developed **authority-level proposed catalog**, not an official DGA, NCA, or SDAIA catalog and not a legal opinion. It translates national obligations and authoritative guidance into practical enterprise architecture principles and standards. Before approval, the Authority must validate the latest versions, applicability, Arabic legal texts, sector-specific obligations, and internal delegations with the relevant EA, cybersecurity, data, privacy, legal, procurement, and digital-government owners.

## 1. Executive Position

This catalog deliberately separates three things that are often mixed together:

1. **Architecture principles** — durable decision rules describing *what the Authority values and how choices should be made*.
2. **Enterprise standards** — testable, enforceable statements describing *what applicable solutions and practices must or should do*.
3. **Authoritative sources** — Saudi laws, policies, controls, and guidelines, plus selected international standards from which the enterprise statements are derived.

The recommended governance hierarchy is:

`Saudi law and national regulatory requirements → sector requirements → Authority policy → EA principles → enterprise standards and patterns → solution requirements and designs → evidence and waivers`

Where a Saudi national requirement conflicts with an international reference, the applicable Saudi requirement prevails. An ISO, NIST, W3C, IETF, OMG, or Open Group standard becomes internally mandatory only when the Authority formally adopts it, a Saudi requirement incorporates it, or a contract/certification requires it.

NORA requires government entities to define and approve architecture principles and related standards as part of the EA framework. The DGA Digital Transformation Basic Standards also call for NORA-compatible EA and evidence of principles with their rationale and implications/consequences. This document is structured to satisfy that practical need.

## 2. Normative Language and Adoption Status

| Term / status | Meaning in this catalog |
|---|---|
| **Shall / Mandatory** | Required for every item within the stated applicability after formal Authority approval. Nonconformance requires an approved waiver unless a stronger external rule prohibits waiver. |
| **Conditional mandatory** | Required when the stated condition applies, such as personal data, cloud, critical systems, public channels, or AI. |
| **Should / Preferred** | Enterprise default. A materially different approach requires documented justification and architecture approval. |
| **May / Permitted** | Allowed within stated conditions but not the default. |
| **Deprecated** | Existing use may continue temporarily under a remediation or retirement plan; no new use without waiver. |
| **Prohibited** | New or continued use is not allowed except where an external authority explicitly approves it. |

## 6. Governance and Operating Rules

### 6.1 Ownership

| Catalog area | Recommended accountable owner | Required contributors |
|---|---|---|
| EA principles, architecture method, models, decisions, waivers | Head of Enterprise Architecture / EA Governance Committee | Strategy, Digital Transformation, IT, Data, Cybersecurity, PMO, business owners |
| Business capabilities, services, journeys, processes | Accountable business/service owners | EA, Digital Experience, Quality, Operations |
| Application and integration standards | Application / Integration Architecture owner | Development, Operations, Cybersecurity, Data Office, suppliers |
| Data standards and sharing | Chief Data Officer / Data Management Office | Data owners/stewards, Privacy, Cybersecurity, EA, Legal |
| Technology, cloud, service-management and resilience standards | IT / Technology Architecture owner | Cloud, Infrastructure, Operations, BCM, Cybersecurity, Procurement |
| Cybersecurity and cryptography standards | Cybersecurity executive / CISO | EA, IT, Data, Privacy, Risk, Internal Audit |
| Privacy standards | Privacy / Data Protection owner | Legal, Data Office, Cybersecurity, business owners, EA |
| AI and GenAI standards | Approved AI governance body / CDO or delegated executive | Business, Data, Cybersecurity, Privacy, Legal, Risk, EA |

### 6.2 Minimum metadata for every standard

Each standard record should contain: unique ID; title; normative statement; domain; rationale; implications; authoritative sources and versions; applicability; adoption status; accountable owner; approvers; effective date; review date; affected architecture object types; compliance tests; evidence examples; related patterns; known exceptions; replacement/supersession history.

### 6.3 Review cycle

- Review the full principles catalog at least every **two years**, or earlier following a material strategy, mandate, national-policy, or operating-model change.
- Review the standards catalog at least **annually** and whenever DGA, NCA, SDAIA/NDMO, sector regulators, or a referenced international standard issue a material update.
- Technical products and versions require more frequent lifecycle review based on vendor support and cyber-risk changes.
- Approved principle changes should be rare. Standards and patterns are expected to evolve more often.

### 6.4 Waiver requirements

A waiver shall include: affected standard and assets; scope; business justification; options considered; consequence of compliance and noncompliance; cybersecurity, privacy, data, continuity, financial, interoperability, and supplier risks as applicable; compensating controls; accountable risk owner; approvals; start and expiry dates; remediation/exit actions; monitoring evidence. A waiver shall never be used to override a non-waivable law or competent-authority requirement.

### 6.5 Architecture gate integration

| Gate | Minimum standards evidence |
|---|---|
| Concept / demand | Strategic outcome, capability/service impact, shared-service and reuse assessment, initial data/security/privacy/AI classification |
| Options / procurement | Current and target architecture, standards applicability, total lifecycle cost, cloud/hosting decision, interoperability and exit requirements |
| Solution design | Principle and standards assessment, decisions, threat/privacy/data assessments, interface contracts, quality attributes, continuity design |
| Pre-production | Test evidence, control evidence, operational acceptance, accessibility, recovery, monitoring, documentation, approved residual risks/waivers |
| Post-implementation | Benefit and service measures, architecture update, issues/debt, lessons, supplier/service performance, waiver follow-up |

## 7. Recommended Initial Adoption Sequence

### Wave 1 — Governance and national compliance foundation

Approve the 22 principles and operationalize S-01, S-03, S-04, S-10, S-17, S-20, S-21, S-23, S-27, S-28, S-29, S-30, S-31, S-33, S-35, and S-36. Create the applicability matrix against current DGA, NCA, SDAIA/NDMO, PDPL, and sector requirements.

### Wave 2 — Reuse, interoperability and service quality

Operationalize S-05 through S-16 and S-32 through S-34. Publish reusable templates for service blueprints, BPMN, API contracts, architecture decisions, quality attributes, and operational readiness.

### Wave 3 — Data and technology rationalization

Operationalize S-18, S-19, S-22, S-24, S-25, and S-26. Link standards to the application, data, technology, interface, and project portfolios; identify noncompliance, technical debt, end-of-support exposure, duplication, and modernization initiatives.

## 9. Approval Notes and Limitations

1. The catalog should be approved as a **separate, version-controlled EA governance artifact** referenced by the EA Policy. Detailed, changing standards should not be frozen inside the high-level policy.
2. The source register is intentionally broader than the 36 actionable standards. The Authority should conduct a formal applicability assessment and may add sector-specific standards, national circulars, contractual obligations, and internal policies.
3. NCA controls, PDPL obligations, and other competent-authority requirements must be interpreted and owned by the designated control owners; EA links and traces them but does not replace their statutory governance.
4. Product-specific standards—approved databases, operating systems, cloud services, development frameworks, endpoint platforms, monitoring tools, and versions—should be maintained in a separate **Technology Standards Catalog** linked to S-24.
5. Detailed solution patterns—API gateway, identity federation, logging, data exchange, cloud landing zone, backup, integration, and AI patterns—should be maintained in a separate **Architecture Patterns Catalog** linked to the relevant standards.
6. Before executive approval, translate the final approved catalog into formal Arabic, validate terminology with the Authority, and preserve bilingual IDs so both versions remain synchronized.
